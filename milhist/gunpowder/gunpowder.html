Gunpowder Era (1500 to 1850)

The Gunpowder Era in military history, spanning roughly from 1500 to 1850, was marked by the growing dominance of firearms, artillery, oceanic empires, and increasingly professional armies. Fortifications evolved to resist cannon, infantry drill became more disciplined, cavalry adapted to new battlefield roles, and naval warfare expanded on a global scale. The battles below trace that transformation from the Italian Wars and Ottoman expansion through the wars of religion, dynastic conflict, revolution, and the Napoleonic age.

The Battle of Marignano (1515)

Italian Wars, France, Swiss, Artillery

Marignano was one of the first great battles of the early gunpowder age, where Francis I's French army used heavy cavalry, artillery, and allied infantry to defeat the feared Swiss pikemen outside Milan.

The Battle of Pavia (1525)

Italian Wars, Habsburg, France, Arquebus

Pavia demonstrated the growing lethality of handheld firearms when Imperial forces shattered the French army and captured King Francis I, ending one phase of the Italian Wars.

The Battle of Lepanto (1571)

Naval, Ottoman, Holy League, Mediterranean

The great galley battle of Lepanto checked Ottoman naval dominance in the Mediterranean and became one of the most celebrated Christian victories of the sixteenth century.

The Battle of Sekigahara (1600)

Japan, Tokugawa, Samurai, Unification

Sekigahara decided the struggle for control of Japan after Toyotomi Hideyoshi's death and paved the way for the Tokugawa shogunate's long rule.

The Battle of Breitenfeld (1631)

Thirty Years War, Sweden, Gustavus Adolphus, Tactics

Breitenfeld made Gustavus Adolphus famous across Europe by showing how mobile artillery and flexible infantry formations could defeat the Habsburg-Catholic League system.

The Battle of Naseby (1645)

English Civil War, Parliament, Royalist, New Model Army

Naseby was the battle that broke the main Royalist field army and made Parliament's New Model Army the dominant force in the English Civil War.

The Battle of Blenheim (1704)

War of the Spanish Succession, Marlborough, France, Bavaria

Blenheim stopped a possible Bourbon victory in central Europe and established the Duke of Marlborough as one of the great commanders of the age.

The Battle of Poltava (1709)

Great Northern War, Russia, Sweden, Peter the Great

Poltava destroyed Charles XII's invasion of Russia and marked the rise of Russia as a major European military power.

The Battles of Saratoga (1777)

American Revolution, Britain, Continental Army, Alliance

Saratoga is often called the turning point of the American Revolution because the British surrender persuaded France to enter the war openly against Britain.

The Battle of Waterloo (1815)

Napoleonic Wars, France, Britain, Prussia

Waterloo ended Napoleon's final bid for power and closed the revolutionary and Napoleonic era with a coalition victory in Belgium.
